# PART XXIII — THE WEALTH BUILDER’S ALMANAC

*Seasonal counsel. Reread when the calendar turns.*

---

## Spring — Planting

Spring is for beginnings that look small.

Open the accounts. Raise the automation by one percent. Plant a skill practice. Have the awkward money conversation you deferred all winter. Clean beneficiaries after life’s quiet changes.

Spring mistake: planting twenty seeds and watering none. Choose fewer plants. Attend them.

Clason’s wisdom is a spring wisdom: keep a portion. Hill’s definite aim is a spring wisdom: name the crop. Clear’s systems are spring irrigation.

Do not wait for perfect weather. Plant in adequate weather with a coat on.

---

## Summer — Growth and Heat

Summer brings income heat for many—and lifestyle heat for all.

Raises arrive. Weddings and travel multiply. Comparison season opens because more skin and more status are on display.

Summer rules:

- Capture income heat into surplus before lifestyle heat becomes permanent  
- Enjoy consciously funded pleasures  
- Do not buy a new identity because the sun is out  
- Maintain the investing automation while you are distracted by living  

Buffett’s temperament matters in summer markets too—when prices are warm and stories are warmer.

---

## Autumn — Harvest and Audit

Autumn is truth.

Pull net-worth statements. Harvest tax documents. Audit fees. Review insurance. Hold the family meeting. Teach. Give according to plan. Kill what did not grow.

Autumn is also when people panic-sell after a bad season or over-celebrate after a good one. Neither ritual replaces process.

Read Graham in autumn. Margin of safety is an autumn idea: winter is coming, always, in some form.

---

## Winter — Preservation and Study

Winter is for solvency and books.

Build reserves if thin. Simplify. Study history of prior winters—1970s, 2008, your era’s latest scare. Strengthen the body. Write the ethical will. Sit with enough.

Seneca belongs to winter. So does Taleb. So does quiet.

Winter mistake: despair that the orchard is bare when it is only dormant. Another winter mistake: burning the orchard for one night of heat—leverage, liquidation, fraud’s false fire.

---

## Leap Years and Rare Years

Some years contain births, deaths, mergers, migrations, windfalls, wars.

In rare years, slow the discretionary life and speed the documentation. Call counsel earlier than pride prefers. Reduce simultaneous large bets. Increase sleep.

Rare years are when families either become legends of competence or case studies in Appendix-less regret.

# PART XXIV — CONVERSATIONS WITH THE DEAD MASTERS

*Imagined counsel, grounded in their known doctrines—used as study, not séance.*

---

## Franklin at the Desk

**Q.** How do I begin?  
**A.** Be industrious at something useful. Be frugal without being mean. Let reputation precede your requests. Write your virtues and score yourself without theatrical self-hatred.

**Q.** What of display?  
**A.** Display spends twice: once in coin, once in the envy you teach others to have of you—and yourself to maintain.

---

## Clason in the Marketplace

**Q.** What if my tenth is too small to matter?  
**A.** Streams cut stone. The habit is the first treasure. Increase later.

**Q.** What of gold that must work?  
**A.** Do not leave all your gold idle, nor send it with strangers into folly. Make it a worker under watch.

---

## Graham on a Volatile Morning

**Q.** The price fell; was I wrong?  
**A.** Price is not value each morning. If you bought a share of a business thoughtfully, Mr. Market’s panic is sometimes a gift. If you bought a ticket to euphoria, you have learned the tuition schedule.

**Q.** How much safety?  
**A.** Enough that being wrong does not end you. The rest is ego.

---

## Bogle on Fees

**Q.** But the active fund might outperform.  
**A.** Might. After costs, most do not for most people over long periods. Own the haystack; stop paying premium rates to hunt needles.

---

## Munger in the Study

**Q.** How do I get smarter at money?  
**A.** Learn mental models across disciplines. Invert—avoid stupidity preferentially. Stay within competence. Deserve good outcomes.

**Q.** What ruins the smart?  
**A.** Leverage, liquor of certainty, and envy. Also: being around people who normalize idiocy.

---

## Stoics on Fortune

**Q.** What if I lose it?  
**A.** You will lose all of it at death. Practice holding goods with a loose hand and a strong character. Prefer self-command to showcase.

---

## Housel at the Kitchen Table

**Q.** Why do I keep sabotaging a good plan?  
**A.** Because you are human. Design for humans: automation, room for error, a life enjoyable enough that you can endure longevity. Perfect math with intolerable psychology fails.
